Races

Races of Aethrya

Forty-seven playable races across three availability tiers — each carrying their own cultural history, divine entanglements, and established role within the world's political and ecological structure. Your race determines your initial relationship to Aethrya's divine politics before your first scene opens.

🧑
Common Races
13 races available at standard whitelist. Humans, dwarves, elves, halflings, gnomes, and more — each embedded in at least one divine network's operational strategy from the moment they appear in the world.
🌙
Limited Races
7 races available in restricted server-wide quantities. Drow, Avariel, Fey'ri, Satyr, Hexblood, Fox Hengeyokai, Yuan-Ti Pureblood. Elevated narrative visibility; post-whitelist application required.
✨
Fabled Races
4 races requiring full Fabled application — Pantheon Race, Cambion, Mind Flayer, True Vampire. The most demanding and most rewarding character options available. Full application process required.

Race Tier Philosophy

The three tiers reflect narrative weight as much as mechanical complexity. Common races are the world's baseline — characters who function naturally within its social structures and whose presence in any setting raises no immediate flags. Limited races are visible departures from that baseline — a Drow in Leilon's market, an Avariel in Emberreach, prompts reactions from every character who encounters them, and those reactions are part of the story. Fabled races exist at a scale of narrative consequence where every scene they enter is shaped by what they represent — and none more so than the Pantheon Race, which represents a hidden god in mortal form.

The application requirements for each tier exist to match the investment they ask of the DM team with the investment they require of the player. A Fabled race character has a Lore Team contact, a prepared story arc, and DM-managed ongoing consequences that no other character type receives automatically. The application process is the covenant that makes that investment sustainable.
