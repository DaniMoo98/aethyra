Consequence Rules

How the world responds to what your character does â€” the living principle that every action in Aethrya carries weight, that institutional memory is long, and that a divine network's patience is not the same as its absence.

Consequences in Aethrya operate on two entirely separate levels. Narrative consequences are IC responses to character actions, managed by the DM team as story developments. Mechanical sanctions are OOC responses to rules violations, managed by the Moderation team. These systems must never be conflated â€” being arrested IC is not a punishment; it is the world responding to what your character chose to do, and responding to it with good RP craft is a mark of narrative maturity.

Narrative Consequences

Every significant IC action carries the possibility of narrative consequence. A character who commits a crime within hub city jurisdiction faces the hub guard response system. A character who makes enemies of a faction will find that faction's considerable resources redirecting toward them. A character who violates a divine institution's norms will find that institution's mortal network responding in ways both visible and â€” more troublingly â€” not.

Consequence severity scales with the severity of the action, its visibility, the target's factional connections, and the character's established record. First-offense consequences for minor infractions may be as light as a formal warning. Repeat offenses, major crimes, or actions against significant NPCs escalate accordingly â€” and the divine networks of Aethrya have memories that span centuries.

Criminal actions within hub cities trigger the Hub Guard Response system immediately. Actions outside hub territories trigger faction-specific responses from any faction with jurisdictional claim over the affected region. Actions with no witnesses and no evidence trail may produce no immediate response â€” but Aethrya's intelligence networks are comprehensive, patient, and long-memoried. The absence of immediate consequence is not evidence that no one noticed.

Actions that fall within a deity's domain may attract divine attention even when they do not constitute Accord violations. A character who repeatedly violates Tempus's principle of honorable engagement will find Aldric Brand's professional network intersecting their path. This is not mechanical punishment â€” it is a DM event hook. Divine attention is always a story opportunity, and in Aethrya, story opportunities are the most valuable currency there is.
