Charm & Dominate Rules

The consent framework governing all mind-affecting spells and abilities — a structure that honours player agency while preserving the genuine narrative power of enchantment magic in a world where Velara Dusk's counseling practice exists.

Charm and dominate effects targeting player characters require OOC consent from the targeted player before narrative consequences are applied. Casting the spell IC proceeds normally. Applying its consequences requires an OOC conversation first.

The Consent Framework

Mind-affecting magic occupies uniquely sensitive territory in collaborative roleplay because it directly constrains a player's control over their own character. Aethrya's framework balances narrative realism — these spells are powerful, and that power should carry genuine story weight — with the foundational principle that no player is ever required to have their character's actions controlled by another without consent.

When a mind-affecting spell succeeds mechanically, the casting player privately requests consent from the targeted player. The targeted player may consent, partially consent (defining what actions they will roleplay under the effect), or decline. If the targeted player declines, the spell still succeeded mechanically — it simply produced no observable narrative effect. The casting character does not know their spell failed to hold, which creates a narratively rich ambiguity that both players can explore.

Subtle Spell Interactions▶

Sorcerer Subtle Spell removes somatic and verbal components — meaning no other characters observe the casting. It does not remove the OOC consent requirement for applying narrative consequences. A Subtle Charm Person still requires player consent. What Subtle does affect is the evidence and investigation layer: no witness can testify to having observed spellcasting, which is exactly how a certain grief counselor in Emberreach's upper districts maintains plausible deniability.

NPC Targets▶

Mind-affecting spells targeting NPCs do not require OOC consent — the DM manages NPC responses as part of the narrative. However, charm or domination of significant NPCs (factional leaders, divine mortal-form figures, named recurring characters) requires DM involvement before and after the spell is cast, as consequences for these actions ripple through the world in ways the DM team needs to manage carefully.
